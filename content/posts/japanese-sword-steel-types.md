Japanese swords have a reputation as some of the finest blades ever made. But the steel they're made from varies enormously.

A $100 wall-hanger and a $500 cutting sword might look similar, but the steel inside determines whether the blade can hold an edge, survive impact, or snap on the first hard target. And the listings don't always make it easy to tell the difference.

This guide covers every steel type you'll see on modern Japanese swords, what each one actually means, and how to choose the right one for how you plan to use it.

## A Quick Note on Tamahagane

Before we get into modern steels, it's worth mentioning the original.

Tamahagane — meaning "jewel steel" — is the traditional material used to forge Japanese swords for over a thousand years. It's made by smelting iron sand with charcoal in a clay furnace called a tatara. It's a labor-intensive process that takes up to 72 hours and produces a bloom of steel with wildly uneven carbon content. The swordsmith sorts the pieces by eye, selects the best material, then folds and forges it repeatedly to drive out impurities and even out the carbon distribution.

It's a remarkable material with deep cultural significance, and it's still produced today in Shimane Prefecture — but only a few times per year, in small quantities, exclusively for licensed Japanese swordsmiths. It's actually illegal to export tamahagane as a raw material.

If you're shopping for a katana today, you're almost certainly choosing between the modern steels below. Tamahagane is a subject that deserves its own deep dive, and we'll cover it separately.

## How to Read Steel Numbers

Before comparing specific steels, it helps to understand the naming system.

Most carbon steels used in swords follow the AISI 10XX convention. The first two digits (10) indicate plain carbon steel with no significant alloying elements. The last two digits represent the approximate carbon content in hundredths of a percent. So 1060 means roughly 0.60% carbon, and 1095 means roughly 0.95% carbon.

Why does that matter? 

Because carbon content is the single biggest factor in how a steel responds to heat treatment. More carbon means the steel can be hardened to a higher level — which means better edge retention and wear resistance. But it also means less toughness and more risk of cracking or chipping if the heat treatment isn't done right.

If you want the full explanation of how [heating and cooling changes steel's properties](/posts/what-happens-to-steel-when-heated-and-cooled/), we've covered that in detail. For this article, just remember: higher number = harder but more brittle, lower number = tougher but softer.

## 1045 Carbon Steel — Entry Level

**Carbon content:** ~0.45%
**Best for:** Display, light decoration, absolute beginners on a tight budget

1045 is the lowest carbon content you'll find on a sword that can reasonably call itself functional. It's tough — hard to break, relatively forgiving of mistakes — but it doesn't have enough carbon to take or hold a particularly sharp edge. It also can't be effectively clay tempered, which means you won't get a real hamon or the hard-edge/soft-spine duality that defines a quality Japanese blade.

1045 swords are cheap, often under $100. That's fine if you want something to hang on a wall or practice basic forms. But it's also the steel that dishonest sellers frequently substitute when they claim a sword is made from something better. If a listing promises 1095 or T10 at a price that seems too good to be true, there's a good chance you're getting 1045 and a marketing story.

## 1060 Carbon Steel — The Workhorse

**Carbon content:** ~0.60%
**Best for:** Functional cutting, martial arts practice, best all-around value

1060 is where functional Japanese swords really begin. It hits the sweet spot between hardness and toughness. There's enough carbon to take and hold a good edge, and enough ductility to absorb impact without chipping or cracking.

A well-made 1060 katana is a legitimate cutting tool. It handles tatami mats, bamboo, and water bottles without complaint, and it's forgiving enough to survive the occasional bad angle or accidental contact with a hard target. For most practitioners — especially those doing regular tameshigiri (test cutting) — 1060 offers the best balance of performance, durability, and price.

It also responds well to clay tempering, which improves the edge hardness while keeping the spine tough. A clay-tempered 1060 is a genuinely capable blade.

Expect to pay $150–500 for a well-made 1060 katana, depending on fittings and finishing.

## 1095 Carbon Steel — The Sharp Edge

**Carbon content:** ~0.95%
**Best for:** Serious cutting performance, collectors who prioritize edge retention

1095 is high-carbon steel. It takes and holds a razor-sharp edge — noticeably sharper than what 1060 can achieve. When it's properly heat-treated, it's one of the best steels available for pure cutting performance.

The tradeoff is brittleness. A poorly heat-treated 1095 blade can chip on hard targets or even crack under lateral stress. This steel has very little margin for error in the forge. But when it's done right and clay tempered, 1095 produces a blade with a hard edge (typically 58–60 HRC) and a softer, tougher spine that can flex without snapping. That differential hardness is what creates a real, functional hamon: the visible wavy line on the blade where hard and soft steel meet.

The hamon on a clay-tempered 1095 blade isn't decorative. It's a visible record of the heat treatment process — a boundary between martensite at the edge and pearlite at the spine. If a seller claims their 1095 blade has a hamon but the sword costs $80, you should be skeptical. Real clay tempering requires skill and adds cost.

Expect to pay $200–600 for a quality 1095 katana.

## T10 Tool Steel — The Premium Choice

**Carbon content:** ~1.0% plus tungsten
**Best for:** Maximum performance, serious collectors and practitioners

T10 is a tungsten-alloyed tool steel, and it's widely considered the best steel available in production-level Japanese swords.

The carbon content is similar to 1095 (~1%), but the addition of tungsten makes it even better. Tungsten increases hardness, improves scratch and abrasion resistance, and adds toughness at high hardness levels. A properly tempered T10 blade can exceed HRC 60 while remaining tougher than a 1095 blade at the same hardness. That translates to better edge retention, more resistance to wear, and a blade that can handle harder targets with less risk.

Like 1095, T10 shines when clay tempered. The combination of T10's inherent properties with differential hardening produces what many sword enthusiasts consider the best functional blade available outside of custom forgework.

The downside: T10 requires more maintenance than lower-carbon steels. It has minimal corrosion resistance, so regular oiling is essential. And T10 swords cost more — typically $300–700 for a well-made production piece.

If you're buying one katana and want the best performance for the money, T10 clay tempered is the answer most experienced collectors will give you.

## 9260 Spring Steel — The Unbreakable Option

**Carbon content:** ~0.60% plus silicon and manganese
**Best for:** Heavy cutting practice, martial arts training, durability over sharpness

9260 is a different philosophy entirely. Where 1095 and T10 optimize for edge hardness, 9260 optimizes for toughness and flexibility.

The defining characteristic of 9260 is its 2% silicon content, which gives the steel extraordinary resilience. A 9260 blade can be bent nearly 90 degrees and spring back to true. That would snap a 1095 blade in half. This makes it virtually indestructible under normal sword use, including hard impacts, bad cutting angles, and the kind of repeated stress that martial arts training puts on a blade.

The tradeoff is edge performance. 9260 won't hold as keen an edge as 1095 or T10, and it won't achieve the same level of hardness. But if your priority is a blade that will survive anything you throw at it — years of heavy tameshigiri, training drills, or demonstration cutting — 9260 is built for that job.

Expect to pay $200+ for a quality 9260 katana.

## 5160 Spring Steel — Tough and Resilient

**Carbon content:** ~0.60% plus chromium
**Best for:** Hard-use blades, impact resistance, outdoor/field use

5160 is another spring steel, but with a different alloying approach. Where 9260 uses silicon for its toughness, 5160 uses chromium — which adds some corrosion resistance and wear resistance on top of the impact toughness.

5160 steel is slightly less flexible than 9260 but still far tougher than any plain carbon steel. It's a popular choice for swords designed for real-world hard use — cutting demonstrations, outdoor practice, or situations where the blade might contact unexpected hard objects.

5160 is also common in western-style swords and large knives, which speaks to its reputation for reliable toughness across the blademaking world.

## L6 Bainite — The Specialist

**Carbon content:** ~0.70% plus nickel, chromium, and molybdenum
**Best for:** Serious collectors, maximum toughness at high hardness

L6 is band saw steel — literally designed to flex millions of times without failing. When heat-treated to produce a bainitic microstructure (rather than the martensite that most sword steels use), L6 achieves something unusual: very high hardness combined with exceptional shock resistance.

Most sword steels force a tradeoff between hardness and toughness. L6 bainite narrows that gap significantly. A well-made L6 blade can hold an edge nearly as well as T10 while absorbing impacts that would chip or crack a harder, more brittle steel.

The catch: L6 bainite is rare in production swords. The heat treatment required to produce a bainitic structure is more complex than standard quench-and-temper, so you'll mostly find L6 in higher-end semi-custom or custom pieces. If you see it listed, you're shopping in the serious practitioner and collector tier.

## Folded / Damascus Steel — The Aesthetic Layer

This one needs some myth-busting.

Historically, folding was essential. Tamahagane came out of the tatara furnace full of impurities and inconsistent carbon. Folding the steel 10–15 times homogenized the carbon distribution, welded out slag inclusions, and refined the grain structure. The visible layered pattern (hada) was a byproduct of solving an engineering problem.

Modern sword steels don't have this problem. A bar of 1095 from a steel mill has consistent carbon content throughout. When a modern sword maker folds 1095 or T10, they're creating the visual pattern — not fixing a structural deficiency.

A well-executed folded blade is beautiful, and the hada pattern gives each sword a unique fingerprint. Just understand what you're paying for. If a seller charges $200 extra for folding and implies it makes the blade stronger or sharper, that's misleading. Folding modern steel is an aesthetic upgrade, not a performance one.

## Clay Tempering: The Process That Matters Most

If there's one thing to take away from this entire article, it's this: clay tempering matters more than steel grade.

Clay tempering — also called differential hardening — is the process that gives a Japanese sword its defining characteristics. The smith applies clay to the spine and sides of the blade before quenching, insulating those areas so they cool slowly while the exposed edge cools rapidly. The edge forms hard martensite (great for cutting), while the spine retains a tougher, more flexible structure (great for surviving impact).

This is also what creates a real hamon — the visible temper line on the blade. A genuine hamon is a physical boundary between two different microstructures. An acid-etched hamon is just a cosmetic line drawn on the surface. They look similar in photos, but only one reflects actual differential heat treatment.

The connection between [temper colors and temperature](/posts/why-does-steel-turn-blue-when-heated/) applies here too — the blue and straw tints that form during tempering are the same thin-film oxide colors you see on any heated steel.

A clay-tempered 1060 will typically outperform a non-clay-tempered 1095. The process matters that much. When shopping, always check whether a blade is clay tempered — it's the single biggest indicator of a functional sword versus a decorative one.

## How to Spot What You're Actually Getting

A few red flags to watch for:

**Exotic steel at rock-bottom prices.** If someone is selling a "T10 clay-tempered folded katana" for $75, you're not getting T10. You're almost certainly getting 1045 with a marketing description written to move units. Quality materials and skilled heat treatment cost money, and there's no shortcut around that.

**Acid-etched hamons.** A real hamon from clay tempering has subtle, natural variation. A fake hamon from acid etching tends to look too uniform or too perfectly wavy. If the listing doesn't specifically mention clay tempering, assume the hamon is cosmetic.

**Stainless steel blades.** Stainless steel contains too much chromium to be effectively hardened for sword use. A stainless steel katana is a display piece. It will fail — sometimes dangerously — if used for cutting.

**Rat-tail tangs.** A quality sword has a full tang — the steel of the blade extends through the entire handle. Some cheap swords weld a thin threaded rod onto the blade at the handle, creating a weak point that can snap.

**No seller transparency.** A reputable seller will tell you exactly what steel the blade is made from, how it was heat-treated, and what construction method was used. If that information isn't available or the answers are vague, move on.

## The Bottom Line

Steel choice is where the conversation starts, but it's not where it ends.

For display or casual handling, 1045 works fine. For functional cutting and training, 1060 is the best value. For maximum edge performance, 1095 clay tempered is excellent. For the best all-around production blade, T10 clay tempered is the standard. For heavy-use durability, spring steels like 9260 and 5160 are purpose-built. And for the serious collector, L6 bainite represents the current edge of what's available.

A well-made 1060 with proper clay tempering and solid construction will outperform a poorly made T10 every time. Steel grade is the foundation. What the smith does with it — the heat treatment, the geometry, the fit and finish — is what makes it a sword.

Pick the steel that matches your use case and your budget. Then focus on finding a maker or seller who's honest about their process. That combination will serve you better than chasing the highest-spec steel in a poorly made blade.
