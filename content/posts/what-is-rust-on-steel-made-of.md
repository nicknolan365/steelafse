Rust is one of the most recognizable things in the material world. The flaky, reddish-brown crust on an old fence, a neglected tool, the underside of a car in the northeast. Everyone knows what rust looks like. Almost nobody knows what it actually is.

It's not dirt. It's not surface contamination. It's not something that lands on steel from the outside. Rust is what steel becomes when iron reacts with oxygen and moisture. It's a chemical transformation of the metal itself.

## The short answer

Rust is iron oxide. Specifically, it's a mixture of hydrated iron oxide compounds that form when iron reacts with oxygen in the presence of water.

Steel is 97–99% iron, which is reactive. When it's exposed to air and moisture, the iron atoms on the surface give up electrons, bond with oxygen, and form a new compound: iron oxide. That compound is the reddish-brown flaky substance you see on corroded steel.

## What rust is actually made of

Rust isn't a single, clean chemical compound with one specific formula. It's a mixture, primarily made up of iron(III) oxide (Fe₂O₃) and iron(III) oxide-hydroxide (FeOOH), along with varying amounts of water trapped in the structure.

The exact composition depends on conditions like temperature, humidity, how long the steel has been exposed, whether salt or pollutants are present. This is why rust can range from a thin orange film to thick, black, layered scale. Different environments produce slightly different mixtures, but they're all variations of the same basic reaction: iron plus oxygen plus water.

## How rust forms

The process is straightforward, but it happens in stages.

First, moisture lands on the steel surface. This can be rain, humidity, condensation, or even the thin film of water that exists on most surfaces exposed to air. That moisture acts as an electrolyte — a medium that allows ions to move.

Once the electrolyte is in place, iron atoms at the surface begin to lose electrons. Those electrons travel through the metal and react with oxygen and water, forming hydroxide ions. Meanwhile, the iron atoms that lost electrons become iron ions, which then combine with oxygen to form iron oxide.

The result sits on the surface as rust. But it doesn't stop there.

## Why rust keeps spreading

This is the part that makes rust so destructive compared to corrosion on other metals.

When aluminum corrodes, the oxide layer that forms is thin, hard, and tightly bonded to the surface. It seals the metal underneath and essentially stops the reaction. Aluminum oxide is protective. The corrosion fixes itself.

Rust does the opposite. Iron oxide is porous, flaky, and loosely bonded. It cracks. It expands — rust occupies more volume than the iron it formed from. As it swells and flakes off, it exposes fresh metal underneath. That fresh metal corrodes, forms more rust, expands, flakes off, and exposes more metal.

It's a self-sustaining cycle. Rust doesn't protect steel. It accelerates the destruction of it.

This is why a small spot of rust on a car can turn into a hole if you ignore it. The corrosion isn't static. It's actively working inward, and every layer it peels away gives the next layer less protection.

## Rust vs burning — same reaction, different speed

Rust is oxidation. So is fire. The chemistry is nearly identical — iron reacting with oxygen to form iron oxide, releasing energy in the process.

The difference is speed. Rust is oxidation happening over days, weeks, or months. It's slow enough that the energy it releases dissipates as low-grade heat you can't even feel. Fire is the same oxidation happening so fast that the energy release is visible — as heat and light.

This is exactly why [steel wool burns even though solid steel doesn't](/posts/why-steel-wool-burns/). The thin strands of steel wool have so much surface area that oxidation happens fast enough to sustain combustion. A steel beam has so little relative surface area that oxidation can only happen at the pace we call rust.

Same reaction. The geometry determines whether it takes months or seconds.

## Why water makes everything worse

Iron can oxidize in dry air, but it happens extremely slowly. Water is the accelerant.

Moisture provides the electrolyte that allows the electrochemical reaction to proceed efficiently. Without it, electrons can't move freely, ions can't form, and the reaction stalls. With it, everything speeds up dramatically.

Salt makes it even worse. Dissolved salt increases the conductivity of the water on the surface, which means electrons move more easily and the reaction rate climbs. This is why cars in northern states with salted winter roads rust far faster than identical cars in dry climates. It's why coastal steel structures need more aggressive corrosion protection than inland ones. And it's why a steel tool left in a damp garage will deteriorate while the same tool kept in a dry drawer stays clean for years.

Humidity alone is enough. Steel doesn't need to be submerged in water to rust — it just needs consistent moisture in the air. In environments above roughly 60% relative humidity, the rate of corrosion increases significantly. In tropical or coastal climates, unprotected steel can show visible rust within days.

## Rust only happens to iron and steel

People use "rust" loosely to describe any metal corrosion, but technically, rust refers specifically to iron oxide. Only metals containing iron can rust.

Aluminum corrodes, but it forms aluminum oxide — a hard, transparent layer that protects the surface. Copper corrodes and develops a green patina (copper carbonate), which is why old copper roofs and the Statue of Liberty are green instead of copper-colored. Bronze, brass, and zinc all form their own oxide layers when exposed to the elements.

None of these are rust. They're different compounds formed by different reactions, and most of them are actually protective. Iron is somewhat unique in that its corrosion product works against it rather than for it.

When people search "rust on metal," they're almost always talking about steel. And they're right to — steel is by far the most common metal that suffers from this specific problem.

## Why some steel rusts faster than others

Not all steel corrodes at the same rate, even in the same environment. Several factors determine how quickly rust takes hold.

**Composition** matters. Carbon steel with no alloying additions is the most vulnerable. Adding at least 10.5% chromium creates stainless steel, which forms a passive chromium oxide layer similar to aluminum's protective coating. But even stainless steel can corrode under the right conditions, which is a topic that deserves its own article.

**Surface condition** plays a role. A polished surface has fewer microscopic sites where moisture can collect and corrosion can initiate. A rough, scratched, or machined surface has more. Mill scale — the dark oxide layer left on steel after hot rolling — can actually promote corrosion rather than prevent it, because it creates galvanic cells between the scale and the base metal.

**Coatings** are the most common defense. Paint, galvanizing (zinc coating), powder coating, and oil all work by creating a physical barrier between the iron and the oxygen and moisture it needs to react. When the coating fails — a scratch in the paint, a chip in the galvanizing — the exposed spot becomes the starting point for rust.

**Environment** ties it all together. The same piece of unprotected steel will last decades in an arid desert and months on a salt-sprayed coastline. Temperature, humidity, salt exposure, and even air pollution all influence corrosion rate.

## Rust is steel trying to go back to where it came from

Iron doesn't exist in nature as pure metal. It exists as iron ore — iron oxide, the same compound that makes up rust. Smelting and steelmaking are energy-intensive processes that force iron into an unstable, pure metallic state.

Rust is that process reversing. The iron is returning to its natural, lower-energy form. In a thermodynamic sense, steel is always trying to become rust. Corrosion protection is just the effort we put into slowing that down.
