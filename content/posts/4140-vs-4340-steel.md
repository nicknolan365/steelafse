If you've ever spec'd a part that needs to be strong, tough, and heat-treatable, you've probably landed on 4140 or 4340. They're two of the most popular alloy steels in manufacturing, and on paper they look similar. Both are chromium-molybdenum steels. Both machine well. Both respond to heat treatment.

So why does 4340 cost more, and when does it actually matter?

## The Quick Difference

The short answer is nickel. 4340 contains approximately 1.65-2.00% nickel, while 4140 has none. That nickel addition gives 4340 better toughness, deeper hardenability, and higher fatigue resistance — especially in larger cross-sections.

## When 4140 Is the Right Call

For most applications, 4140 is the better value. It's widely available, machines well in the annealed condition, and can be heat treated to impressive strength levels (up to about 250 ksi tensile). If your part is moderate in size (under ~3" cross-section), doesn't see impact loading, and operates at normal temperatures, 4140 will do the job.

Common 4140 applications include gears, shafts, bolts, tooling, and structural components.

## When You Actually Need 4340

The nickel in 4340 earns its keep in a few specific scenarios:

- **Large cross-sections** where you need through-hardening. 4140 starts to lose hardenability past about 3 inches; 4340 maintains it much deeper.
- **High fatigue environments** like aircraft landing gear, connecting rods, or components that see millions of load cycles.
- **Impact and shock loading** where toughness at high strength levels is critical.

If your part is small, doesn't see cyclic loading, and doesn't need to be hardened past a few inches deep, you're paying a premium for properties you won't use.

## The Bottom Line

Start with 4140. Move to 4340 when the application specifically demands deeper hardenability, better fatigue life, or higher toughness at elevated hardness. The material cost difference is real, and in high-volume production, choosing the right grade saves serious money without compromising the part.
